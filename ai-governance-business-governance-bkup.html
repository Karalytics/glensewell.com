AI Governance Isn't an AI Problem—It's a Business Governance Problem

By Glen Sewell | Published May 2026

Organizations often approach AI governance as a technology challenge. In reality, successful AI governance begins with business accountability, risk management, decision rights, and governance structures that already exist across the enterprise.

When people hear that I work in AI governance, they usually assume my days are filled with algorithms, model validation, explainability techniques, architecture diagrams, product roadmaps, operating models, and complex technical controls.

Sometimes they are.

But after more than twenty years working in data, analytics, governance, and now AI, I've come to believe something different.

The adoption of AI doesn't fundamentally change how businesses manage risk. It simply reveals whether the foundations already in place are ready for an AI-enabled world.

The more I work with clients, the more I see the same pattern emerge. Many assume AI requires an entirely new way of governing their business. New committees. New operating models. New policies. New technologies.

Some of those things may eventually be necessary. But they're rarely the best place to start.

Long before anyone was talking about generative AI or large language models, businesses were already asking familiar questions.

  • Who owns this decision?
  • Who is accountable if something goes wrong?
  • How are risks evaluated?
  • Who approves new initiatives?
  • How do different business functions work together?
  • How are decisions documented and monitored over time?

None of those questions are unique to AI.

AI simply raises the stakes. It reveals whether the underlying foundations of your business are capable of supporting the next generation of technology.

Start with Your Existing Foundations

Rather than creating entirely new governance structures, I encourage clients to first evaluate what already exists. Risk management, privacy, information security, compliance, records management, data governance, internal audit, and model risk management often provide much of the foundation needed for responsible AI.

The challenge is rarely that these capabilities don't exist. More often, they exist independently rather than as an integrated foundation supporting AI adoption.

Governance Starts with People

When clients ask me which AI framework they should adopt or which technology platform they should implement, I usually encourage them to pause and ask a different set of questions first.

  • Who owns this AI capability?
  • Who decides whether the risk is acceptable?
  • Who is accountable for the outcome?
  • Who monitors performance once it's deployed?

Technology can support those conversations. It can't replace them.

Governance Enables Innovation

Another misconception is that governance slows innovation.

I've never believed that.

Poor governance slows innovation because important decisions become emotional debates. Good governance accelerates innovation because everyone understands the objectives, the decision rights, and their role in achieving them.

The businesses making the most meaningful progress with AI aren't necessarily the ones investing in the newest models. They're the ones that have built strong foundations—clear accountability, effective governance, thoughtful leadership, and business processes that people understand and trust.

Final Thought

In many ways, AI governance feels remarkably familiar. The technology continues to evolve at an incredible pace, but the principles that enable organizations to succeed haven't changed nearly as much.

Clear accountability. Thoughtful leadership. Transparent decision-making. Effective risk management. Strong data practices. Trust.

Those principles mattered before AI. They matter today. And they'll continue to matter long after the next wave of innovation arrives.

That's why I don't see AI governance as simply a technology challenge. I see it as a reflection of organizational readiness.

Organizations that recognize this won't just be better prepared to adopt AI responsibly. They'll be better prepared for whatever comes next.
